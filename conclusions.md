# Conclusions

## Summary of Results

| Analysis | Region | SST on Axis 1 | Axis 1 Variance | Outcome |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Rutterford et al. (2023) | NE Atlantic | R²=0.890 | 29% | Original claim |
| **Reproduction (DATRAS)** | NE Atlantic | R²=0.573 | 29.2% | **Confirmed** |
| **Replication (ClimateFish)** | Mediterranean | R²=0.615 | 45.2% | **Confirmed** |
| **Replication (MEDITS shelf)** | Mediterranean | R²=0.367 | 18.9% | **Confirmed** |

## Key Findings

1. **The claim is reproducible.** Using the same DATRAS data source and methodology, SST emerges as the primary driver of PCoA Axis 1 with nearly identical variance explained (29.2% vs 29%).

2. **The claim generalises to the Mediterranean.** Two independent Mediterranean datasets — ClimateFish (visual census) and MEDITS (trawl surveys) — both confirm SST as the primary driver when restricted to shelf depths.

3. **The claim is specific to continental shelf communities.** Without depth filtering, trawl surveys spanning wide depth ranges (e.g., MEDITS 10-800m) show depth as the dominant driver. The SST signal only emerges when analysis is restricted to shelf depths (≤200m).

4. **Effect size varies with SST range.** The SST R² decreases from 0.890 (NE Atlantic, 10°C range) to 0.573 (NE Atlantic reproduction, 9°C) to 0.367 (Mediterranean, 5.6°C), consistent with a weaker signal where temperature varies less.

## Implications

- Rutterford et al.'s finding is robust: SST is the primary environmental driver structuring fish communities on continental shelves across the NE Atlantic and Mediterranean.
- Future projections of fish community change under climate warming should account for the shelf-specificity of this relationship.
- The depth filter is essential when using trawl survey data that spans shelf-to-slope depths.
