Demo Scenario: From Biodiversity Dataset to Replication-Tracked Scientific Knowledge
Title: Bio-ORACLE meets Science Live — replicating the SST-fish community claim in the Mediterranean

The Problem
Bio-ORACLE is one of the most widely used marine biodiversity datasets in the world — 32,000+ users, 1,500+ citations, used globally for species distribution modelling. But despite its reach, three things are missing:

No Croissant metadata — ML frameworks like PyTorch, TensorFlow, and JAX cannot automatically discover or load it. It is invisible to tools like Google Dataset Search in the ML context.
No structured claims — the scientific knowledge derived from it exists only in thousands of scattered papers, not as independently verifiable, machine-readable assertions.
No replication tracking — among 1,500+ citing papers, many have independently tested the same claims, but there is no structured way to see whether the original findings hold across species, regions, and timeframes.


Step 1 — Make Bio-ORACLE ML-ready with Croissant
We describe Bio-ORACLE's sea surface temperature (SST) layer using the Croissant metadata format — a JSON-LD file that tells ML tools:

What the dataset contains
How to load it into PyTorch / TensorFlow / JAX
Its spatial resolution (0.05 degrees, ~5.5km globally)
Its temporal coverage (2000–2100, 10 decadal steps)
Its license and provenance
Its CMIP6 climate scenario variants (SSP1, SSP2, SSP5)

This Croissant file is published on Hugging Face, making Bio-ORACLE's SST layer discoverable by Google Dataset Search for the first time in an ML context.


Step 2 — Create nanopublications on Science Live

We create a chain of five nanopublications on Science Live, following the same pattern
used for the DGGS, ZKP, and WeatherXBiodiversity showcases.


--- Nanopub 1: Paper Quotation ---

Source paper: Rutterford, L.A., Simpson, S.D., Bogstad, B., Devine, J.A. & Genner, M.J. (2023).
             Sea temperature is the primary driver of recent and predicted fish community
             structure across Northeast Atlantic shelf seas.
             Global Change Biology, 29(9), 2510-2521.
             DOI: 10.1111/gcb.16633

Quotation:  "In conclusion, our study clearly identifies sea temperature as the primary
             driver of fish community composition across the Northeast Atlantic
             continental shelf."

Comment:     Rutterford et al. analysed standardised abundance data for 198 marine fish
             species across 23 fisheries trawl surveys (31,502 sampling events, 2005-2018)
             from southern Spain to northern Norway. Their PCoA analysis showed SST was the
             environmental variable most closely associated with the first axis of variation,
             explaining 29% of variance (R²=0.890). Rutterford et al. used Bio-ORACLE
             (Assis et al., 2018; DOI: 10.1111/geb.12693) for their future SST projections.
             Bio-ORACLE v2.0 provides global marine environmental layers at 5 arcmin
             (~9.2 km) resolution, with temperature layers validated against 445,248 in situ
             quality-controlled records (Pearson's r = 0.99, MAE = 0.39°C).

                 structure across Northeast Atlantic shelf seas.
Domain:          Marine ecology / Fish community ecology

--- Nanopub 4: FORRT Replication Study ---

Replication study: Fouilloux, A. (2026). Replicating the SST-fish community relationship
                   in the Mediterranean Sea using ClimateFish and Bio-ORACLE.

Original study tested:  Rutterford et al. (2023), DOI: 10.1111/gcb.16633

Replication approach:   We test whether sea surface temperature is also the primary driver
                        of fish species abundance in the Mediterranean Sea, using:
                        - ClimateFish database (Azzurro et al., 2022): abundance data for
                          15 indicator fish species (7 indigenous + 8 non-indigenous) from
                          3,142 visual census transects across 7 Mediterranean countries
                          (2009-2021), openly available via SEANOE/OBIS
                        - Bio-ORACLE v2.0 SST layers for environmental data
                        - Same analytical approach: community analysis (PCoA on Bray-Curtis
                          similarity) + regression against environmental variables

Relationship to original: Independent test of the same claim (SST as primary driver)
                          in a different region (Mediterranean vs. NE Atlantic),
                          using a different data source (visual census vs. trawl surveys),
                          with a different species set (climate indicator species vs.
                          full demersal community).

Data sources:
  - ClimateFish: https://www.seanoe.org/data/00756/86784/ (CC-BY 4.0)
    Paper: Azzurro et al. (2022), Frontiers in Marine Science, DOI: 10.3389/fmars.2022.910887
  - Bio-ORACLE: https://bio-oracle.org/ (Assis et al., 2018)
  - Code repository: https://github.com/annefou/bio-oracle (TBD)


--- Nanopub 5: FORRT Replication Outcome ---

Outcome:     Confirmed
Direction:   Consistent with original claim
Summary:     Our PCoA analysis of 15 climate indicator fish species across 28
             Mediterranean sites (aggregated from 2,222 ClimateFish visual census
             transects, 2009-2021) confirms that sea surface temperature is the primary
             driver of fish community structure. SST was the environmental variable most
             strongly associated with PCoA Axis 1, which explained 45.2% of community
             variation (r=-0.784, R²=0.615, p<0.001). Salinity was secondary (r=-0.472,
             R²=0.222, p=0.011), and depth dominated Axes 2-3. This is consistent with
             Rutterford et al.'s NE Atlantic finding (SST: R²=0.890 on Axis 1, 29%
             variance). The lower R² in our study likely reflects the narrower SST range
             in the Mediterranean (17-23°C) compared to the NE Atlantic (9-18°C), and
             the smaller species pool (15 vs 198 species).
Scope note:  The replication uses a different region (Mediterranean vs NE Atlantic),
             different data collection method (visual census vs trawl surveys), and a
             different species set (15 climate indicator species vs 198 demersal fish),
             yet reaches the same conclusion.

Step 3 — Replication pipeline

Similar to the WeatherXBiodiversity and DGGS replication pipelines, we build a
reproducible analysis that re-tests the claim independently. The full pipeline
is containerised in a Docker image published to GitHub Container Registry for
reproducibility.

| Step | Script                    | Input                          | Output                              |
|------|---------------------------|--------------------------------|-------------------------------------|
| 1    | 01_download_climatefish.py| SEANOE / OBIS API              | data/climatefish_med.csv            |
| 2    | 02_download_biooracle.py  | Bio-ORACLE (sdmpredictors)     | data/biooracle_sst_med.nc           |
| 3    | 03_spatial_join.py        | ClimateFish + Bio-ORACLE       | data/fish_environment_joined.csv    |
| 4    | 04_community_analysis.py  | Joined data                    | results/pcoa_axes.csv               |
| 5    | 05_variable_importance.py | PCoA axes + environment        | results/regression_table.csv        |
|      |                           |                                | results/variable_importance.png     |

Pipeline details:
  1. Download ClimateFish abundance data (15 species, 7 countries, 2009-2021)
  2. Download Bio-ORACLE environmental layers for the Mediterranean:
     SST (mean), sea surface salinity, depth, and optionally chlorophyll-a
  3. Spatial join: match each ClimateFish transect to Bio-ORACLE grid cell
     environmental values based on coordinates
  4. Community analysis: PCoA on Bray-Curtis similarity matrix of species
     abundance across sites (following Rutterford et al.'s methodology)
  5. Variable importance: multiple linear regression of PCoA axes against
     environmental variables (SST, salinity, depth) with stepwise AIC selection
     → Does SST explain the largest proportion of community variation?

Key question: Does SST emerge as the primary driver of fish community structure
in the Mediterranean, as Rutterford et al. (2023) found for the NE Atlantic?


Step 4 — The connection: Croissant, Science Live, and FORRT linked together

The Croissant metadata file includes a field pointing to the Science Live nanopublication
as the claim derived from the dataset. The original claim nanopub links to its replication
nanopubs. The replication nanopubs link back to both the original claim and the FORRT entry.

For the first time, anyone can go from:

"I want to train a species distribution model" → find Bio-ORACLE via Croissant on Hugging Face
"I want to know what this dataset proves" → follow the link to the Science Live claim
"Has this claim been replicated?" → see the replication — tested for Mediterranean fish
"I want to verify the replication" → the full pipeline is open, citable and reproducible

Why it matters

A marine biologist, fisheries manager, or conservation NGO can now:

Find the Bio-ORACLE dataset automatically via ML tools
Understand what it proves: SST drives fish community structure (NE Atlantic)
See whether the claim holds in the Mediterranean — our replication tests this
Trust the evidence chain because it is transparent, independently verifiable, and replication-tracked
Build on it knowing exactly what conditions and species the claim has been tested for
